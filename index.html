A History

Hi! I'm Tyler, I'm a non-binary (they/them) person originally from Sacramento-ish, California, and lately based in London, England. I have over a decade and a half of experience helping the average person understand the digital tools at their disposal. That's looked like working as a tutor, adjunct professor, customer support agent, developer relations, event production manager, software engineer, and more — none of those are in the order you think they're in — and some of you may be saying already that most of those don't sound like jobs where I'd be doing a lot of education about tech, but the joke would be on you! Everyone needs to understand their technology.

I was radicalized (is that the right word?) to that idea early on, and in a couple different ways: first, because I was homeschooled as a kid, my dad thought it would be a good idea to have me learn HTML and CSS. That came in super handy when MySpace launched and suddenly all of my friends needed help building out their home pages with auto-playing music and sparkling Top-8s. One day a friend of mine said, "Wouldn't it be cool if we had our own MySpace?" So I went to my dad and asked how to do that; he handed me books on PHP and MySQL, and after a couple weeks of homeschooler hyperfocus I had a fully fleshed out social network for my friends. Now, luckily I didn't think to save any screenshots because I'm sure everything we posted was just layers of cringe, but overwhelmingly I thought "Wow, people can just do this. Why doesn't everyone do this?"

When I was early on in learning about computers, I was gifted one of those netbooks for kids — you know, the ones they made before everyone had an ipad in class — that ran a bespoke, "simplified" Linux OS. I didn't know anything about Linux, so I trial-and-errored my way through things like running Minecraft or setting up email, but at some point I needed a bit of software installed via the terminal, and I couldn't figure out how to do it. There weren't any instruction manuals with the OS, and even typing "help" into the command line returned loads of unrelated topics. I turned to the internet and everything I found pointed back to the same guide, which said:

To install a program on your Linux machine, simply:

  1. Locate the URL of the program you want to install
  2. Install as you would on any other Linux device

Imagine little 13-year-old me screaming at the website, "But what is that???" (turns out it was yum install if you're curious) And ever since, any time I find an unhelpful document or an "Explain it like I'm 5" video that uses too many big words, I'm transported back to that moment. And this happens a lot! There is a thing we see in tech over and over again which is that people (let's be honest: tech bros) think they need to look smart, and the best way to do that is to confuse everyone around them. These guys think that if no one can understand them, people will just trust that they're some kind of unknowable genius. Unfortunately, a lot of the time they're right (look at certain electric car CEOs), but that doesn't mean that all of us have to fall for their tricks. There are better ways.

And that brings us to now, some twenty-odd years later, still working to make the things that should be simple... simple.

Digital DIY

Your computer, phone, tablet, watch, doorbell, fridge, car, etc., are super-powered devices of sheer brilliance and wonder.

They are also, unfortunately, controlled by some of the worst people on Planet Earth.

"Controlled?" I hear you cry, "That's a pretty strong statement, don't you think?" And, yeah, it is, but consider: when you plug your phone in at night, it probably checks for software updates. Who makes that software? Do you? What's to stop the people who do make the software from making the nightly update... permanently disable your device? If you paid for the device outright, or pay monthly through your cell plan, they have no direct contract with you that prevents them from doing that. And given that the most powerful owners of the biggest companies are cozying up to increasingly fascist government leaders, they could do so without any repercussions that matter. What are you going to do, call your lawyer? With what phone?

The same goes for the websites you use, which is where I come in.
